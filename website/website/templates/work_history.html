Pathways Intern - Flight Software Systems Branch

NASA Langley Research Center

Hampton, Virginia
May 2018 - Present

This summer I worked on ARCSTONE, or Achieving Instrument High Accuracy In-Orbit. ARCSTONE is a CubeSat science instrument which aims to develop a model of lunar irradiance as it relates to solar irradiance to provide an additional absolute reference for past, present, and future earth sensing instruments. Currently, ARCSTONE is at Technology Readiness Level (TRL) 4 and must be tested in a relevant environment before it can upgrade to TRL 5. To accomplish this, a system had to be developed to aggregate test data from ARCSTONE as well as auxiliary DSLR cameras into a science data product for the scientists to use and publish.

The system I developed, ARCSTONE Field Test System (FTS), in Python uses a GUI to display real-time baseline information from ARCSTONE’s spectrometers as well as real-time images from additional DSLR cameras that attempt to document test details such as the amount of cloud coverage over the lunar target and the image frame ARCSTONE’s spectrometers are receiving through the telescope. Additionally, to ease the execution of the field tests, ARCSTONE FTS had a requirement of being able to command, update registers, and retrieve setting information from the ARCSTONE instrument and auxiliary DSLR cameras. To accomplish this, I implemented a central thread that focused on updating GUIs and sending commands. Furthermore, I implemented an individual thread for each sensor which acted as state machine with the possibility of sitting idle or executing continuous capture.

This system went under a formal review with 4 full-time NASA engineers. This was a great learning experience for me on safe practices when using queues, multiple threads and processes. Additionally, I wrote an official Architecture and Functional Design document for ARCSTONE FTS that was included in the formal review. I really enjoyed getting the opportunity to create a useful application that is currently being used by many members of the ARCSTONE mission team.

Variable Drag System Software Design Lead

River City Rocketry

University of Louisville
August 2016 - January 2018 [1 year 6 months]

River City Rocketry (RCR) is University of Louisville's (UofL) rocketry team which competes in NASA's Student Launch (NSL): a yearly competition designed to prepare college students for the Human Exploration and Operations workforce. When joining the team before the start of the Fall semester in 2016, I began with simple tasks such as updating our Variable Drag System's (VDS) Arduino sketch to actuate the motor completely at an altitude of 350m in order to test the drag blades' friction coefficient. Soon I began to pick up additional responsibilities and quickly rose to the position of Software Design Lead for the VDS subsystem which at the time was led by Ben Stringer.

During my first semester with the team, I focused on designing the data acquisition system (DAQ) for the VDS. Since Ben and I were both new to working with microcontrollers and microcomputers, the VDS went through several design phases which consisted of a wide variety of hardware. We worked with Arduinos, Beaglebone Blacks, and Raspberry Pis before deciding on a Teensy 3.6. This drove me to study large amounts of documentation sheets as I was responsible for configuring the sensors and controllers to communicate with each other. Furthermore, I led the design and implementation of the software from gathering data from sensors, to deducing information, and storing data.

During the Fall Semester, we had several test launches. We were able to use the flight data gathered by the DAQ system to work on improving our sensor frequency, reducing the noise displayed in the data and also analyze other aspects of the flight after launch. I designed the DAQ system in both Python and C/C++ for use on the Raspberry Pi or the Teensy 3.6. The state of the program at the time I left in the winter for my first internship at NASA JSC can be found on my GitHub at https://github.com/jtcass01/VDS2_0. This repository includes proofs of some computations we used to derive information such as velocity using two unique sources (altitude and acceleration).

After I left in December, Ben implemented the motor actuation and altitude detection components of the VDS. In the following April, during competition, RCR received the honor of first place in NSL. The Variable Drag System I helped design worked incredibly well, forcing the rocket to apogee at only 22 feet over our 1 mile target (percent-error of ~0.4%). We also received an award for having the best vehicle design but I cannot take any credit for that. It was a true privilege to work with such a talented group of individuals, and we are far from done yet.

After finishing my first USRA internship at JSC in the Spring of 2017, I returned to UofL to attend class and work on paving the way for RCR's future. After an ambitious year, many of us were still hungry for more. We set out to design a two-staged, 15 foot rocket to launch at the end of the summer. My role was to design a telemetry system capable of ranges over 10,000 feet as well look ahead to the future of the Variable Drag System and the improvements we could make as it wasn't likely that Ben would return to the team when he finished his internships at Raytheon.

To design the telemetry system, I first looked to popular forums such as Reddit and Stack Overflow. From these forums, I got tons of information on how other's developed similar systems for their hobby weather balloons. This gave me a great starting point and a community to reach out to during the initial development stages. In the end, I found DigiKey to be a very reliable and trusted manufacturer of RF sensor modules that could be used for transferring data. After purchasing two RF modules, designing a printed circuit board, and picking out a microcontroller for the ground station and a microcomputer for on board the rocket, Nolan Holden, a pathways intern with the US Navy, and I designed a telemetry test for the two-staged rocket flight.

Our goal was to test the range of the RF modules and how transmittance speed is deteriorated by increases in distance. Sadly during the preparation for our launch, one of our antennae was severed while putting the rocket together. We were able to solder the sever but with a severely gimped range.

Now, during the Spring of 2018, we have upgraded the VDS's IMU to a VectorNav 100 which provides a much higher frequency of data. These improvements are pushing our team to implement multithreading and multiprocessing to handle these large data sets without hindering our response time. We have upgraded our previous microcontroller (Teensy 3.6) to a Beaglebone Black to give us more CPUs and an operating system to ease the control of the different cores and microcontroller on board the Beaglebone.

USRA Intern - Spacecraft Software Engineering Branch

NASA Johnson Space Center

Houston, Texas
August 2017 - December 2017 [5 months]

During the Fall of 2017, I worked with the Spacecraft Software Engineering Branch (ER6) at JSC in support of Orion MPCV's Ascent Abort 2 Test (AA2). My project, along with another intern, was to develop an application for decoding and analyzing the Electronic Data Recorders jettisoned during the descent of AA2. Our task was to calculate bit error rates as well as retrieve parameters and work towards plotting different parameters from the data.

Since we were the first interns to start the project, we had the luxury of choosing the programming language and eventually designing the program's control flow. We choose Python as our programming language due to the power of its libraries to sift, sort, and apply computations to large matrices while still having a relatively easy to work with GUI library. Since I had experience decoding byte streams after working on a RF telemetry system for my university's rocket team and the other intern had experience designing Java GUIs, we agreed to split the work up with me focusing on the back-end data processor and him focusing on the front-end GUI.

The project required me to analyze every byte of multiple sets of 2-4 GBs of data, each carrying data from the same flight recorded at different places on the vehicle, and compare the results to mask any individual errors in the dataset. Due to the large amount of processing required, the project drove me to learn about multithreading, multiprocessing, CPU and memory usage and how to make them the most efficient. Furthermore, to increase processing speed, I was able to utilize several third party libraries such as Pandas and NumPy for handling the data and Ctypes for calling sections of C code from Python.
